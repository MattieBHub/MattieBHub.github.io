The effect of the Coronavirus on student learning and their outcomes

During the COVID-19 pandemic, education around the world was significantly impacted. Teachers and Students couldn't attend classes and had to learn to learn online, which raised many questions about lack and quality of learning experienced during this time. This project aims to investigate school closures due to the pandemic, focusing on the UK, and how grades were awarded when students weren't allowed to go in for exams.

UK schools first closed all doors nationwide mid-March 2020. Figure 1 studies when this was in the overall timeline of the pandemicColab. Figure 1 is created using the covid19api.com api and shows that this was relatively near the start of the pandemic.

Different countries reaction times to the pandemic can be viewed using the Figure 2, showing that globally in-person education was mostly totally stopped for long stretches of time in 2020, shown as earlier in pandemic when viewed in tandem with Figure 1, and opened up a lot more in 2021 and 2022.

Map Colab

The UK students missed a vast amount of teaching time when schools first shut and online teaching was being developed, missing a total of 110 in classroom days (Figure 3b) and effectively missing 61 days of school time between March 2020 and April 2021 (Figure 3a), which is nearly a 1/3 of a normal 190 day school year.

With all this teaching being lost to the pandemic, soon came the question of how were the examinations going to be handled. Ofqual intially attempted to award 'calculated' grades based on a rank order of students within the Grade boundaries, when this failed to satisfy public opinion they awarded the students their predicted grades.

Figure 4 demonstrates that their was a sudden jump from around 67% to 76% of English students who got 4/C and above in the years when their exam grades were given. This trend continues into 2021 and may have had lingering effects as 2022's grades are comparatively higher then the years before the pandemic. Further research could investigate reasons behind this.

Colab where I scraped the data for Figure 4

Figure 5 shows in more detail the grade distribution in the years surrounding and encompassing the years when the grades where given. Their is slight increase in the highest grades but bigger increases are witnessed in mid-range grades 4-6. There is also decreases in proportion of 1-3 between 2020-2021, perhaps suggesting that teachers knew that the predicted grades would be given, compared to 2020 where they thought they were going to be awarding 'calculated' grades', so boosted their students grades. Further research could investigate this. Figure 4 & 5 show that more students got higher grades after & during years effective and during the COVID-19 pandemic, despite previous graphs showing they got less school time then previous years.

Conclusions:

Overall, COVID-19 has had a significant impact on global education and has a presented a number of challenges to the UK education system. Despite missing more school then previous years, students who took exams, or were supposed to, got higher percentage of students and higher grades. This suggests that the giving of predicted grades, perhaps inflated the amount of people who passed their exams compared to previous years. These findings can inform future research on possible inflation of grades and lasting effects of COVID-19 on education.
